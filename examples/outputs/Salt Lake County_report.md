## Executive Summary

# Executive Summary: Salt Lake County Land Assessment

Analysis of geospatial data from 1990 to 2024 indicates a consistent trend of urban expansion and land cover transformation within Salt Lake County. The most prominent change is the growth of developed land, which increased from approximately 21.3% of the total land area in 1990 to 33.14% by 2024. This transition is characterized by a steady rise in barren and impervious surfaces, reflecting the conversion of natural or semi-natural landscapes into residential, commercial, and industrial infrastructure.

Spatial patterns observed in Sentinel-2 imagery and Landscape Change Monitoring System (LCMS) data reveal a sharp geographic division. The central valley is defined by a dense urban grid and industrial zones, particularly in the northwest near the Great Salt Lake. In contrast, the eastern and western boundaries of the county maintain a high degree of vegetative cover associated with mountainous terrain. While the urban core has expanded, the surrounding natural areas remain subject to episodic fire activity. Monitoring Trends in Burn Severity (MTBS) data confirms that fire events are a recurring factor in the county’s landscape dynamics, with major records occurring periodically throughout the 34-year study period.

The cumulative data illustrates a landscape increasingly defined by human development. The expansion of impervious surfaces in the valley floor contrasts with the stable but fire-prone vegetation of the highlands. These findings provide a baseline for understanding the long-term trajectory of land use and the spatial distribution of environmental disturbances within Salt Lake County.

## Salt Lake County - LCMS Land Cover

### Salt Lake County - LCMS Land Cover

Between 1990 and 2024, the primary land cover trend in Salt Lake County was the steady expansion of **Barren or Impervious** surfaces and a concurrent decline in **Grass/Forb/Herb** cover. Impervious surfaces increased from **22.19%** to **33.04%**, surpassing grass-based cover to become the second-most prevalent class in the county. During the same period, Grass/Forb/Herb cover decreased from **23.73%** to **15.10%**. This inverse relationship suggests a consistent conversion of open or rangeland areas into developed surfaces.

**Tree** cover remained the most stable and dominant land cover type, maintaining a consistent presence of approximately **32% to 33%** throughout the 34-year study period. However, **Water** cover experienced a notable reduction, declining from a peak of **6.61%** in 1990 to **3.75%** in 2024. Other categories, including **Shrubs** and various vegetation mixes, remained relatively minor components of the landscape, with Shrubs decreasing slightly from **4.27%** to **3.08%**.

Spatial analysis of the imagery reveals clear geographic concentrations for these transitions:
*   **Urban Infill and Expansion:** The growth of impervious surfaces (maroon/purple) is concentrated within the Salt Lake Valley, moving progressively from the central urban core toward the southern and western boundaries of the county.
*   **Hydrological Change:** The reduction in water cover is localized to the northwestern corner of the study area, showing a visible recession of the Great Salt Lake shoreline over time.
*   **Topographic Constraints:** Tree cover (dark green) is almost entirely restricted to the high-elevation terrain of the Wasatch Range in the eastern portion of the county. This area shows high spatial stability with minimal encroachment from other land cover classes.

## Salt Lake County - LCMS Land Cover Transitions

### Salt Lake County - LCMS Land Cover Transitions

Analysis of the Landscape Change Monitoring System (LCMS) data for Salt Lake County reveals a primary trend of expanding **Barren or Impervious** surfaces, largely at the expense of **Grass/Forb/Herb** and **Shrub** classes. While the **Trees** class remained the most stable land cover, maintaining approximately 28–30% of the total area across all time steps, it experienced a slight decline from 29.92% in 2010 to 28.44% in 2024.

The expansion of developed and barren land is the most consistent transition identified in the matrix:
*   **Barren or Impervious** cover increased from 17.8% in 2000 to 20.99% in 2010, reaching 23.76% by 2024.
*   The most significant contributor to this growth was the **Grass/Forb/Herb** class, which saw 4.2% of the total county area transition to Barren or Impervious between 1990 and 2000, followed by 3.62% (2000–2010) and 4.69% (2010–2024).
*   By 2024, the total area of stable **Grass/Forb/Herb** cover had declined to 11.67%, down from 17.73% in 2000.

Other notable shifts include a gradual reduction in **Water** surface area, which decreased from 5.44% in 1990 to 3.92% in 2010, and further to 3.52% in 2024. Transitions within the shrub categories were also present; **Shrubs** showed a decrease in stability, with 0.73% of the total area transitioning to Barren or Impervious surfaces in the final period (2010–2024). Overall, the data indicates a steady conversion of vegetated and hydrological features into impervious or barren land classes over the 34-year period.

## Salt Lake County - LCMS Land Use

### Salt Lake County - LCMS Land Use

Between 1990 and 2024, the most pronounced trend in Salt Lake County was the steady expansion of **Developed** land. This category increased from 21.55% to 33.14%, representing a total growth of 11.59 percentage points. By 2024, Developed land emerged as the primary land use category in the county, slightly exceeding Forest cover for the first time in the recorded period.

The increase in development occurred alongside a consistent decline in **Agriculture** and **Other** land uses:
*   **Agriculture** decreased from 9.58% in 1990 to 1.74% in 2024, a reduction of approximately 82% of its initial share.
*   **Other** land cover fell from 12.84% to 9.21% over the same period.
*   The rate of agricultural loss was most rapid between 1990 and 2010, with the decline slowing slightly in the subsequent decade.

In contrast, **Forest** and **Rangeland or Pasture** categories remained relatively stable. Forest cover began the period at 32.31% and ended at 33.12%, maintaining a consistent range with only minor inter-annual fluctuations. Similarly, Rangeland or Pasture showed minimal net change, starting at 23.7% in 1990 and concluding at 22.75% in 2024. These two categories combined continue to represent over 55% of the county's total land area, despite the ongoing urbanization of agricultural and miscellaneous lands.

## Salt Lake County - LCMS Land Use Transitions

### Salt Lake County - LCMS Land Use Transitions

Between 1990 and 2024, Salt Lake County experienced a consistent expansion of **Developed** land, which grew from approximately 21.3% of the total area in 1990 to 32.75% in 2024. This growth was primarily driven by the conversion of **Agriculture** and **Rangeland or Pasture**. The transition to Developed land was most pronounced in the 2000–2010 interval, where 2.27% of the county's total area shifted from Agriculture to Developed and 2.52% shifted from Rangeland to Developed.

The **Agriculture** class saw the most significant reduction in footprint over the 34-year period. Occupying 9.46% of the county in 1990, it declined to 7.1% in 2000, 4.22% in 2010, and reached a low of 1.8% by 2024. While the majority of this loss is attributed to urban development, a smaller portion transitioned to **Rangeland or Pasture** (e.g., 1.02% of the total area between 2010 and 2024).

In contrast, natural land cover classes remained relatively stable:
